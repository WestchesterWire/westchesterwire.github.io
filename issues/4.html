🚨 Breaking

The Epstein depositions come to Chappaqua

By the Wire Staff  ·  Friday, Feb. 27

A town famous for apple orchards and quiet train-platform celebrity sightings just became the center of one of the biggest political stories in the country. Hillary Clinton testified Thursday, February 26 before the U.S. House Oversight Committee — behind closed doors — at Chappaqua Crossing, the sprawling former Reader's Digest campus that's served as her home base for years.

Today — Friday, February 27 — it's Bill's turn. He becomes the first former U.S. president in over a century to testify before Congress. Also at Chappaqua Crossing. Also closed-door. The committee is examining the Clintons' ties to the late Jeffrey Epstein as part of its broader investigation into Epstein's network.

Hillary's statement from Thursday was characteristically measured. Whether that moves the needle for the committee — or the public — remains to be seen. Bill's testimony, whatever it is, won't be public immediately. But this is a historic moment happening right here in our backyard, at a building most locals know as the place that used to have a great holiday market.

"I had no idea about their criminal activities. I do not recall ever encountering Mr. Epstein." — Hillary Clinton, Thursday testimony

For Chappaqua, a village of about 1,400 people in the Town of New Castle, this is a surreal week. National media, security details, and committee staff have descended on a town that normally gets attention only when a Clinton is spotted at their local coffee shop. The Clintons have lived on Old House Lane for decades — it's been a quiet arrangement that locals have largely accepted as part of the landscape.

Today's testimony won't be public. But the fact that it's happening here — in our county, on a campus built by a magazine that once symbolized optimistic American storytelling — is not lost on anyone paying attention.

🏛 Politics & Government

Extra eyes on your church, synagogue, and mosque for the next two months

Westchester County Police and their law enforcement partners announced Wednesday, February 25 that they're ramping up patrols at houses of worship countywide for the next two months. The timing covers Ramadan, Passover, Lent, and Easter — a stretch of overlapping religious observances that brings more people to places of worship across the county.

There's no specific threat driving this — it's a proactive measure the county has taken before during high-attendance religious seasons. If your congregation has security concerns or wants to coordinate with local law enforcement, this is a good time to make that call. Details are up at westchestergov.com.
