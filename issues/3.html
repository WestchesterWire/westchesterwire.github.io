💼 Business & Economy

Your Con Ed bill just went up — and you already owe back pay

Multiple sources  ·  Wednesday, Feb. 25

If you felt a little lighter in the wallet this month, there's a reason. The Public Service Commission unanimously approved Con Edison's three-year rate plan on January 22 — but the new rates are retroactive to January 1. That means you've been billed at 2025 rates for the first three weeks of the year, and Con Ed is coming to collect the difference.

Your electric bill is going up roughly 3.5% this year — about $5 to $7 more per month for the average Westchester household. Gas is climbing 4.4%, or about $10.67 more per month. Over three years, electric goes up roughly 9% total, gas about 6%.

Here's the thing, though — it was supposed to be much worse. Con Ed originally asked for a 13.4% electric increase and a 19% gas increase. The PSC cut that request by nearly 90%. A big reason? The Westchester Municipal Consortium — a coalition of 40 Westchester towns, villages, and cities — banded together to fight the hike. They negotiated transparency requirements forcing Con Ed to hold annual meetings with local officials to review capital investment plans, compare spending in Westchester versus New York City, and address storm preparedness and infrastructure. Scarsdale Mayor Justin Arest said the coalition "demonstrated the strength of a unified voice in advocating for fairer energy costs."

"With this new increase, I don't know how I am going to survive with only one job." — Westchester resident, via News 12

What's driving the increase? Mostly property taxes — Con Ed estimates it'll pay over $3.2 billion in property taxes in 2026, and that gets passed straight to your bill. Add in $17 billion in infrastructure upgrades over three years, clean energy mandates under New York's climate law, and leak-prone pipe replacement on the gas side.

The proceeding drew 20,000 public comments — a record — and over 70 elected officials signed a letter opposing the hike. State Senator Shelley Mayer, who hosted a "No Love for Con Ed" rally on Valentine's Day last year, is now pushing a bill to limit retroactive utility billing. For now, keep an eye on your next statement — the true-up is coming.

🚨 Public Safety

If you speed on the Sprain, the DA has a message for you

Westchester District Attorney Susan Cacace and New York State Police have launched D.A.S.H. — District Attorney's Safer Highways — a stepped-up enforcement initiative targeting the Sprain Brook Parkway and Interstate 684 through the end of February. This isn't your standard speed trap. The DA's office has embedded two assistant district attorneys with State Police Troop K, reviewing cases in real time and flagging the worst offenders for criminal charges. As Cacace put it: "Slow down, or we'll do it for you." The program produced its first arrest on day one — a Connecticut parolee who led troopers on a chase up the Sprain before bailing out on foot in Greenburgh.

The numbers behind the initiative are stark. DWI arrests in Westchester are up 41% since 2020. Car crashes countywide have risen 10% over five years. In 2024, troopers issued more than 800 speeding citations just on the six-mile stretch of the Sprain in Greenburgh — and 37,000+ speeding tickets were written countywide. And if you get caught, it's going to hurt more than it used to — New York's updated DMV point system took effect February 16, bumping a 1–10 mph over ticket from 3 points to 4 and lowering the license suspension threshold from 11 points in 18 months to 10 points in 24 months. I-684 alone carries 70,000+ vehicles a day. Drive accordingly.

🏛 Politics & Government

Mount Kisco just handed its police department to the county — and it's saving millions

Instead of running its own police force, the Village of Mount Kisco has formalized an intermunicipal agreement with Westchester County for police services — a deal that saves taxpayers $2.4 million over the first five years. Under the agreement, the county will assign 25 personnel to Mount Kisco: 17 patrol officers (with a minimum of two on patrol at all times), 3 detectives, and 5 supervising sergeants. The village also gets access to the county's specialized units, investigative services, unified dispatch, and ongoing training.

County Executive Ken Jenkins called it a smart model: "Policing is expensive... By working together, we are saving taxpayer dollars, maximizing existing County resources." Mount Kisco Mayor J. Michael Cindrich agreed, saying the deal "provides exceptional law enforcement services, at considerable cost savings." For Westchester's smaller municipalities struggling with rising costs, this could be the template — shared services that actually work.
